### Summary Assessment *
The thesis asks a behaviourally well-motivated question — whether salient information shocks move professional managers' risk-taking, and whether a decision-support signal moderates that response — and operationalises "shock" through a novel composite index. Domain-wise, the framing in affect/salience/overreaction terms is appropriate and the comparator synthesis (Tables 3.1/3.2) is a genuine attempt to locate the gap rather than merely list prior work. The headline H1 association (SC_total → NRS) is plausible and behaviourally interpretable. My principal reservation is measurement: the dependent variable is a single-item, study-specific 7-point scale, and although the author defends parsimony (Bergkvist & Rossiter, 2007; line 323), a single self-reported intention item cannot be checked for internal consistency or test–retest stability, which leaves the construct doing a great deal of unverified work. Secondary concerns are an under-theorised research gap (the comparator tables show *what* is missing but less *why* it matters theoretically) and several citation inconsistencies that, while individually minor, accumulate into an integrity concern for a final thesis. The contribution is real but currently positioned around an intervention that did not work rather than around the validated association and instrument that did. Major Revision.

## Strengths *

### S1: Behaviourally coherent operationalisation of "information shock" *
Rather than a binary event dummy, the author builds a continuous, theory-linked index from article count, sentiment extremity, attention intensity, and event severity — dimensions that map onto established behavioural channels (salience, affect heuristic, attention-driven trading à la Barber & Odean). This is a more sophisticated operationalisation than most of the comparator literature and is the thesis's strongest domain contribution.

### S2: Honest comparator mapping that locates a real gap *
Tables 3.1/3.2 systematically compare prior systems on sentiment integration, human-participant validation, advisory/XAI design, and portfolio-outcome assessment. The synthesis correctly identifies that the closest comparators (e.g., the Lim entries, lines 644–645) lack professional human-participant validation and portfolio-outcome linkage — a defensible niche for this study.

### S3: Appropriate self-awareness about the single-item choice *
The author does not hide the single-item DV; it is explicitly justified via doubly-concrete-construct parsimony arguments (Bergkvist & Rossiter, 2007) and the practical constraint of administering eight scenarios to time-constrained professionals (line 323). Naming the trade-off is the right scholarly instinct, even if the justification needs strengthening (W1).

## Weaknesses *

### W1: Single-item NRS leaves construct validity largely unverified *
**Problem**: H1 rests entirely on a single-item, study-specific 7-point "Net Risk Stance" measure (line 323). The Bergkvist & Rossiter (2007) defense applies to *doubly concrete* constructs; "intended direction and intensity of exposure adjustment in response to a shock" arguably bundles two things (direction *and* intensity) and is more abstract than the brand-attitude objects that literature addresses. With one item there is no internal-consistency check, no test–retest, and no convergent-validity evidence against an established risk-tolerance instrument (e.g., Grable & Lytton; DOSPERT-style financial-risk scales).
**Why it matters**: If the DV's validity is uncertain, so is everything built on it — including the strong H1 result and the H2 portfolio simulation, which is *derived from* NRS-weighted returns. A committee will reasonably ask whether SC_total predicts a real behavioural disposition or an artefact of a bespoke item.
**Suggestion**: (a) Add convergent-validity evidence if any second risk measure was collected; (b) if not, strengthen the theoretical case that NRS is doubly concrete and add the manipulation-check / usefulness items already in the panel as partial validity anchors; (c) explicitly bound H1/H2 claims to "stated risk intention," not realised behaviour (coordinate with Reviewer 3).
**Severity**: Major

### W2: The research gap is documented but under-theorised *
**Problem**: The comparator tables establish an *empirical* gap (no system combines sentiment + professional validation + portfolio outcomes) but the manuscript does less to establish the *theoretical* stake — i.e., which behavioural theory predicts that a structured shock signal *should* reduce bias, and therefore why a null is theoretically interesting.
**Why it matters**: Without a theoretical prior, the null intervention result reads as a disappointment rather than a test of a named mechanism (e.g., debiasing-via-salience vs algorithm aversion). The contribution is weaker than it needs to be.
**Suggestion**: Add a short theoretical section stating the predicted mechanism by which the dashboard *should* moderate behaviour, then frame the null as evidence on that mechanism's (in)effectiveness in professionals. This converts a weakness into the paper's most novel claim.
**Severity**: Major

### W3: Contribution is positioned around the intervention that failed, not the association that held *
**Problem**: The abstract/title and much of the discussion foreground the bias-reduction intervention, which the data do not support, while under-selling the robust, behaviourally meaningful H1 association and the reusable shock-measurement instrument.
**Why it matters**: This mis-positioning understates the thesis's actual domain contribution and over-exposes it to the (fair) criticism that the intervention did nothing.
**Suggestion**: Re-anchor the contribution on (i) a validated continuous shock index and (ii) evidence that shock intensity systematically relates to stated risk intention, with the dashboard's null as a bounded, honest secondary finding. (Coordinated with the EIC's reframing point; the domain rationale is that the measurement contribution is what advances the field.)
**Severity**: Major

### W4: Citation inconsistencies undermine reference integrity *
**Problem**: Several verified inconsistencies:
- **Duplicate "Lim (2026)"** for two distinct works (lines 644 and 645) with no a/b disambiguation — APA requires "Lim, 2026a / 2026b."
- **Author-name inconsistency for the ICC source**: "Koo & Mae, 2016" (line 1361) vs "Koo & Li, 2016" (line 1566) — these cite the same ICC-reporting guideline; the correct attribution is Koo & Li (2016).
- **Year inconsistency for Doronila**: in-text "Doronila, 2024" (line 323) vs reference list "Doronila, R. A. (2025)" (line 1755), where the title itself reads "…still in 2024?" — three different "2024/2025" signals for one source.
**Why it matters**: Individually trivial, but in aggregate they read as insufficient proof-reading of the scholarly apparatus and will cost marks on the "Research Integration" written criterion. The Koo & Mae/Li slip is the most serious because it mis-attributes a methodological authority.
**Suggestion**: Reconcile all three; standardise in-text and reference-list years; disambiguate the two Lim works; verify the second author of the ICC guideline (Koo & Li, 2016).
**Severity**: Minor

## Detailed Comments *

### Literature Review / Theoretical Framework
- Coverage of the sentiment/NLP-for-finance and decision-support strands is good; the affect/salience grounding is appropriate. Tables 3.1/3.2 are a strength. The gap argument needs the theoretical layer described in W2.
- Consider engaging algorithm-aversion / advice-utilisation literature explicitly, since the null intervention result is most naturally explained there (this also serves Reviewer 3's perspective).

### Methodology / Research Design (domain lens only)
- The single-item DV is the domain-critical design choice; see W1. The eight-scenario, time-constrained-professional rationale is reasonable but should not substitute for validity evidence.

### Discussion
- The discussion should dialogue more with the behavioural literature on *why* a salient debiasing signal might fail among experts (expertise-based overconfidence, reactance, algorithm aversion). This is where the domain contribution can be deepened.

### References
- See W4. Otherwise APA style is applied consistently.

## Questions for Authors *

1. Beyond Bergkvist & Rossiter (2007), what evidence supports treating the single-item NRS as a valid, unidimensional measure of risk-adjustment intention — was any convergent measure or manipulation check available to triangulate it?
2. Which behavioural mechanism predicted that the Shock Score dashboard would *reduce* bias, and given that prediction, how should the null be interpreted theoretically (ineffective signal vs. algorithm aversion vs. ceiling on stated intentions)?
3. The two "Lim (2026)" entries (lines 644–645) describe different systems — are these two separate works, and if so how should they be disambiguated and attributed?
